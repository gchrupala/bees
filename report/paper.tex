\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Communication in animal species takes many forms, from the chemical
signals of ants to the symbolic, compositional language of
humans. Each of these communication systems has its own idiosyncratic
properties. What ecological and evolutionary pressures shaped these
systems has been of substantial interest to biologists and cognitive
scientists.  Communication in general assumes some kind of shared code
between senders and receivers. The emergence of such a code
requires some degree of alignment between these two roles.
Likewise, any change or modification to the code needs to be
coordinated between senders and receivers if the breakdown of
communication is to be avoided.

In many cases it is possible to think of a plausible pathway for the
coordinated emergence and development of a shared code, but a verbally
formulated scenario leaves many details underspecified and makes it
hard to be precise about the specific conditions that make code change
trajectories feasible. For this reason computational models of the
evolution of communication systems can provide insights which are hard to
obtain by other means. Such models have been used extensively to study
the emergence of communication and language (\Cref{sec:related}). In
this paper we model a transition of a particularly intriguing
communicative system in invertebrates: the waggle dance of honey bees.

Communication is referential when signals stand for entities or states in the
world rather than merely expressing the signaler's internal state. The
communicative code of Apis species \cite{vonFrisch1967DanceLanguage} is a unique
referential system where food sites discovered by foraging scouts are indicated
to other members of the colony by means of a figure-of-eight dance whose axis
encodes the direction of the food source, and whose duration stands for its
distance. Different species of Apis vary in the specific dance variant used to
encode the direction of the food patch: in species with exposed horizontal combs
(\emph{Apis florea} and \emph{Apis dorsata}) the bees orient the axis of the
waggle dance directly towards the food patch. In other members of the Apis
genus, combs are vertical and in some species also hidden in cavities. Such
combs make direct pointing impossible, and these species use gravity as a
reference point, and map the azimuth of the food patch relative to the sun onto
the angle of the waggle dance with respect to the vertical. \Cref{fig:waggle}
illustrates these two versions of the waggle dance.\footnote{ This a somewhat
simplified overview: in individual species there may be considerable behavioral
flexibility in how the dance is performed: e.g.\ in Apis mellifera, sometimes
bees dance on a horizontal platform at the entrance to the beehive and this case
they use direct pointing, even though this species generally employs the gravity
code \citep{Esch2012}.}

The direct-pointing variant is iconic:
the form of the signal, the direction of the waggle axis, mirrors the direction
of the referent. The gravity-referenced variant is more abstract, relying on an
indirect mapping of the sun's azimuth onto an angle measured from the vertical.
The waggle dance thus offers a rare opportunity to study how a referential code
can shift from an iconic to a more abstract form.

The distribution of comb orientation and waggle dance code throughout the Apis
phylogeny (\Cref{fig:phylo}) suggest that the horizontal comb and direct
pointing are the ancestral state (extant in stem Micrapis subgenus). Vertical
combs and the gravity-referenced code are derived traits, present in the other
two Apis subgenerat. Additionally, species within the \emph{Apis} sensu stricto
subgenus are cavity nesters. This pattern indicates that the
gravity code was linked to vertical combs and that cavity nesting is not
required for its emergence \citep{https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1365-3113.2009.00504.x}.

Nevertheless, the exact sequence and nature of evolutionary events that led to
the emergence of the gravity-based waggle dance is not known in detail. It is
also not clear how the transition from one version of the code to the other
could happen gradually, while maintaining communicative success at all stages,
and how this process interacted with the change in the orientation of
honeycombs. Because the code is shared, such a transition poses a coordination
problem: a change adopted by senders but not matched by the receivers'
interpretation, or the reverse, disrupts communication, so that intermediate
stages risk a loss of communicative success.

The present study investigates this transition by means of a
computational model of evolving populations of bee-like agents. We
model selection at the level of colonies, and individual behavior at
the level of worker bees as colony members. We address two questions:
under what ecological conditions a direct-pointing referential code
emerges and stabilizes, and what conditions permit a gradual
transition from this code to its gravity-referenced variant. Our aim is
not to reconstruct the precise historical sequence of these changes,
but to delineate the conditions under which such a transition can
proceed without a breakdown of communication.

Our starting point is colonies with horizontal combs, which show some
genomic variation in traits controlling the inclination to direct
signaling and attending to signals. Here we show that communication via
direct pointing evolves readily when the food distribution makes food
moderately hard to find via random search alone.

In the second stage we introduce extrinsic evolutionary pressure for
vertical combs, which degrades the signal of the direct-pointing
code. We show that in this scenario, the following factors correlate with
 the evolutionary transition towards gravity referenced code:
\begin{itemize}
    \item the magnitude of the benefit of vertical combs
    \item the mutation rate
    \item the strength of the coupling between the signaling behavior
      between senders and receivers
\end{itemize}

The pattern of results from these simulations indicates that a basic
referential code based on iconic, direct pointing signal evolves
readily given favorable ecological conditions. On the other hand, the
transition from this basic ancestral system  to the more abstract code
based on gravity referenced pointing only happens under a narrow set
of circumstances involving the confluence of several key favorable
ecological and evolutionary factors.

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Communication in agent societies}
A long line of computational work has studied how communication
systems can emerge and stabilize in populations of interacting agents.
The signaling game introduced by \citet{lewis1969convention} provides
the formal foundation for much of this work, framing a convention of
meaning as a solution to a recurring coordination problem between a
sender and a receiver. \citet{skyrms2010signals} shows how such
signaling conventions can arise without prior agreement through
evolutionary and reinforcement-learning dynamics. Agent-based models
in this tradition have demonstrated that populations playing repeated
language games can self-organize shared, grounded vocabularies and
categories \citep{steels2011modeling}. Laboratory experiments on
iterated learning further show that languages transmitted repeatedly
across learners become progressively more learnable and structured
\citep{kirby2008cumulative}. More recently, deep multi-agent
reinforcement learning has been used to show that discrete
communication protocols can emerge when agents are trained on
cooperative referential tasks \citep{lazaridou2017multi}.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
Our  model is inspired by bee colonies and their waggle dance, but it is
simplified in many important ways, as our goal was not to model bee biology in
detail but rather investigate scenarios for the emergence and evolution of
referential communicative codes, adopting the case of the waggle dance as a way
to ground the modeling in the real world and ensuring that it evolutionarily
plausible.

We model evolution at the level of colonies, and behavior at the level of
individual bee-like agents. There is no sexual reproduction: colonies reproduce
without genetic input from other colonies. Colonies have a number of heritable
traits which are transmitted to offspring colonies subject to mutation, and
individual agents express these traits with some non-heritable individual
variation.

\subsection{Colonies, workers, and traits}
\label{sec:traits}
A colony is characterized by a small set of heritable, real-valued traits.
Five of these govern the behavior of its workers: the \emph{directional bias}
$b$, which controls how precisely a dance points; the \emph{receiver attention}
$a$, the propensity to attend to a dance rather than search at random; the
\emph{sender transposition} $t_s$ and \emph{receiver transposition} $t_r$, which
control the degree to which a worker relies on the gravity-referenced code
rather than the direct-pointing code when producing and interpreting signals,
respectively; and the \emph{search limit} $\ell$, the maximum distance a worker
is willing to travel. Two further traits describe the colony's nest:
the \emph{comb tilt} $\gamma \in [0,1]$, where $\gamma = 0$ is a horizontal comb
and $\gamma = 1$ a vertical one, and the \emph{comb orientation} $\phi$, the
azimuthal heading of the comb. The behavioral traits $b$, $a$, $t_s$, $t_r$ are
bounded to $[0,1]$ and $\ell$ to $[0, D]$, where $D$ is the maximum search
distance.

Workers express the colony traits with non-heritable individual variation. A
worker $i$ realizes each behavioral trait $x \in \{b, a, t_s, t_r, \ell\}$ as
\begin{equation}
  x_i = \mathrm{clip}\bigl(x + \varepsilon_i\bigr), \qquad
  \varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \eta^2),
\end{equation}
where $\eta$ is a fixed worker-variation scale (scaled by $D$ for the search
limit) and $\mathrm{clip}$ returns the value to its admissible range. The comb
traits $\gamma$ and $\phi$ are properties of the colony's nest and are shared by
all its workers.

\subsection{Comb geometry and the two codes}
\label{sec:geometry}
The comb is a flat surface on which dances are performed. Its tilt $\gamma$ maps
to a physical angle $\theta = \gamma\,\pi/2$ from the horizontal, and together
with its orientation $\phi$ it defines a unit normal
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{n} = (\sin\theta\cos\phi,\; \sin\theta\sin\phi,\; \cos\theta).
\end{equation}
A horizontal comb ($\gamma=0$) has $\mathbf{n} = (0,0,1)$; a vertical comb
($\gamma=1$) has a horizontal normal pointing along $\phi$. A signal is an angle
measured within the comb plane, and the two codes correspond to two reference
directions a worker can use to anchor that angle.

In the \emph{direct} (iconic) code the worker projects the world-space direction
of the food, $\mathbf{f}_d = (\cos d, \sin d, 0)$ for food azimuth $d$, onto the
comb plane and reads off its in-plane angle. The reliability of this code is the
length of the projection,
\begin{equation}
  s_{\mathrm{dir}} = \sqrt{1 - \sin^2\theta\,\cos^2(d-\phi)},
\end{equation}
which equals $1$ on a horizontal comb (the food direction lies entirely in the
plane) but shrinks on a tilted comb, vanishing for food directions aligned with
the comb normal. In the \emph{gravity-referenced} code the worker instead
projects the vertical (gravity) direction $\mathbf{g} = (0,0,1)$ onto the comb
plane to obtain a reference angle, and encodes the food azimuth measured
relative to the sun azimuth. Its reliability,
\begin{equation}
  s_{\mathrm{grav}} = \sin\theta = \sin(\gamma\,\pi/2),
\end{equation}
is exactly the complement of the direct code's: it is zero on a horizontal comb,
where gravity is normal to the surface and offers no in-plane reference, and
grows to $1$ as the comb becomes vertical. The two codes are thus geometrically
complementary: the horizontal comb supports only direct pointing, while a fully
vertical comb makes the gravity reference maximally available. When a channel's
strength is below $1$, the recovered angle is correspondingly degraded, modeled
as a circular interpolation between the true angle and a uniformly random one
with weight equal to the strength.

\subsection{Producing and interpreting signals}
\label{sec:signals}
A worker that has found food produces a dance by combining the two codes. Let
$\delta_{\mathrm{dir}}$ and $\delta_{\mathrm{grav}}$ be the in-plane angles given
by the direct and gravity codes for the food just visited. The intended dance
direction is their circular mean, weighted both by the worker's sender
transposition and by each channel's geometric strength,
\begin{equation}
  \delta = \mathrm{circmean}\bigl(
    \delta_{\mathrm{dir}},\, (1-t_{s,i})\,s_{\mathrm{dir}};\;
    \delta_{\mathrm{grav}},\, t_{s,i}\,s_{\mathrm{grav}}
  \bigr).
\end{equation}
A worker with $t_{s,i}=0$ therefore signals purely by direct pointing, one with
$t_{s,i}=1$ purely by the gravity reference, and the weighting ensures that a
code with no geometric support contributes nothing regardless of $t_s$. The
emitted signal is then drawn from a von Mises distribution centered on $\delta$
with concentration $\kappa = b_i\,\kappa_{\max}$, plus a small wrapped Gaussian
dance noise. The directional bias $b$ thus controls the sharpness of the dance:
a low bias yields a near-uniform, uninformative signal, while a high bias yields
a tightly concentrated one.

A worker interpreting a dance performs the inverse operation, decoding the
observed signal under both codes and recombining the two recovered world
directions by a circular mean weighted by $(1-t_{r,i})\,s_{\mathrm{dir}}$ and
$t_{r,i}\,s_{\mathrm{grav}}$, with added interpretation noise. Successful
communication therefore requires senders and receivers to use compatible codes:
a dance produced under the gravity reference is only correctly understood by a
receiver who also decodes it that way.

\subsection{Foraging and colony payoff}
\label{sec:foraging}
A colony is evaluated over a number of independent foraging episodes. Each
episode places a fixed number of food sites at random azimuths and distances,
each with an angular width, a value $v$, and a finite capacity, and samples a
sun azimuth. The colony then makes a sequence of foraging attempts. On each
attempt a worker is drawn at random; if at least one dance has already been
deposited in the episode, the worker follows a randomly chosen dance with
probability equal to its attention $a_i$, decoding it into a search direction,
and otherwise searches in a uniformly random direction. The worker locates the
nearest food site whose direction falls within its angular width of the search
direction and whose distance is within the worker's search limit $\ell_i$, if any
such site with remaining capacity exists.

Payoff accrues per episode. A successful forage yields the site's value $v$ less
a travel cost proportional to the site's distance, and causes the worker to
deposit a dance (advertising the site to later foragers) at a cost
$c_{\mathrm{base}} + c_{\mathrm{cue}}\,b_i$ that increases with the precision of
the dance. A failed forage costs travel proportional to the full search limit
$\ell_i$, and each act of attending to a dance incurs an attention cost. The raw
episode payoff is the sum of food values minus travel, dance, and attention
costs. This payoff is finally scaled by a vertical-comb factor
\begin{equation}
  1 + B\,h(\gamma),
\end{equation}
where $B$ is the magnitude of the vertical-comb benefit and $h$ is either the
identity (a linear benefit in tilt) or a threshold at $\gamma \ge 0.8$; this term
represents an extrinsic ecological advantage of vertical combs that is unrelated
to communication. A colony's fitness is its mean episode payoff (floored at a
small positive value). For diagnostic purposes we also record a
\emph{recruitment advantage}, the difference between the success rate of workers
that followed a dance and that of contemporaneous workers that searched at
random while dances were available; a positive value indicates that the code is
functioning.

\subsection{Evolutionary dynamics}
\label{sec:evolution}
Evolution proceeds in non-overlapping generations over a fixed-size population of
colonies. In each generation every colony is evaluated, and the next generation
is produced by repeated fitness-proportional (roulette-wheel) selection of
parents, whose traits are copied subject to mutation. Each trait is perturbed by
an independent Gaussian step of standard deviation $\sigma_m$ (scaled by $D$ for
the search limit and by the orientation period for $\phi$) and then clipped to
its range. New workers are regenerated from the offspring colony's traits as in
\Cref{sec:traits}.

The mutations of the sender and receiver transposition traits are not
independent: they are drawn as a correlated Gaussian pair with correlation
coefficient $\rho$,
\begin{equation}
  \Delta t_s = z_1, \qquad
  \Delta t_r = \rho\,z_1 + \sqrt{1-\rho^2}\;z_2, \qquad
  z_1, z_2 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_m^2),
\end{equation}
which preserves the marginal mutation scale of each trait while tuning how
tightly the two co-vary. This coefficient $\rho$ controls the coupling between
sender and receiver behavior: when $\rho$ is high, a mutation that shifts senders
toward the gravity code tends to shift receivers by the same amount, so the two
sides of the code can change in a coordinated fashion rather than drifting
apart. The comb tilt $\gamma$ mutates only when comb evolution is enabled;
otherwise it is held fixed.

\subsection{Horizontal comb}
The first version of the model keeps combs horizontal, fixing $\gamma = 0$ and
disabling comb-tilt evolution, with no vertical-comb benefit ($B = 0$). By the
geometry of \Cref{sec:geometry} the gravity reference then has zero strength
($s_{\mathrm{grav}} = 0$), so only the direct-pointing code is functional and the
transposition traits are effectively inert. We use this version to investigate
the conditions under which a direct-pointing referential code emerges and
stabilizes from an initial population with little communicative ability (low
directional bias and attention, and no transposition).

\subsection{Evolution of vertical combs}
In the second version we add an extrinsic selective pressure for vertical combs
by setting $B > 0$, and enable evolution of the colony-level comb-tilt trait
$\gamma$. As $\gamma$ increases under this pressure, the direct channel degrades
while the gravity channel becomes available, so maintaining communicative
success requires the population to shift from the direct to the
gravity-referenced code. Because that shift must be matched on both the sender
and receiver sides, it constitutes the coordination problem of interest, and we
use this version to investigate how the benefit magnitude $B$, the mutation rate
$\sigma_m$, and the sender--receiver coupling $\rho$ govern whether the
transition proceeds without a breakdown of communication.
