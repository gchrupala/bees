\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Communication in animal species takes many forms, from the chemical
signals of ants to the symbolic, compositional language of
humans. Each of these communication systems has its own idiosyncratic
properties. What ecological and evolutionary pressures shaped these
systems has been of substantial interest to biologists and cognitive
scientists.  Communication in general assumes some kind of shared code
between senders and receivers. The emergence of such a code
requires some degree of alignment between these two roles.
Likewise, any change or modification to the code needs to be
coordinated between senders and receivers if the breakdown of
communication is to be avoided.

In many cases it is possible to think of a plausible pathway for the
coordinated emergence and development of a shared code, but a verbally
formulated scenario leaves many details underspecified and makes it
hard to be precise about the specific conditions that make code change
trajectories feasible. For this reason computational models of the
evolution of communication systems can provide insights which are hard to
obtain by other means. Such models have been used extensively to study
the emergence of communication and language (\Cref{sec:related}). In
this paper we model a transition of a particularly intriguing
communicative system in invertebrates: the waggle dance of honey bees.

Communication is referential when signals stand for entities or states in the
world rather than merely expressing the signaler's internal state. The
communicative code of Apis species \cite{vonFrisch1967DanceLanguage} is a unique
referential system where food sites discovered by foraging scouts are indicated
to other members of the colony by means of a figure-of-eight dance whose axis
encodes the direction of the food source, and whose duration stands for its
distance. Different species of Apis vary in the specific dance variant used to
encode the direction of the food patch: in species with exposed horizontal combs
(\emph{Apis florea} and \emph{Apis dorsata}) the bees orient the axis of the
waggle dance directly towards the food patch. In other members of the Apis
genus, combs are vertical and in some species also hidden in cavities. Such
combs make direct pointing impossible, and these species use gravity as a
reference point, and map the azimuth of the food patch relative to the sun onto
the angle of the waggle dance with respect to the vertical. \Cref{fig:waggle}
illustrates these two versions of the waggle dance.\footnote{ This a somewhat
simplified overview: in individual species there may be considerable behavioral
flexibility in how the dance is performed: e.g.\ in Apis mellifera, sometimes
bees dance on a horizontal platform at the entrance to the beehive and this case
they use direct pointing, even though this species generally employs the gravity
code \citep{Esch2012}.}

The direct-pointing variant is iconic:
the form of the signal, the direction of the waggle axis, mirrors the direction
of the referent. The gravity-referenced variant is more abstract, relying on an
indirect mapping of the sun's azimuth onto an angle measured from the vertical.
The waggle dance thus offers a rare opportunity to study how a referential code
can shift from an iconic to a more abstract form.

The distribution of comb orientation and waggle dance code throughout the Apis
phylogeny (\Cref{fig:phylo}) suggest that the horizontal comb and direct
pointing are the ancestral state (extant in stem Micrapis subgenus). Vertical
combs and the gravity-referenced code are derived traits, present in the other
two Apis subgenerat. Additionally, species within the \emph{Apis} sensu stricto
subgenus are cavity nesters. This pattern indicates that the
gravity code was linked to vertical combs and that cavity nesting is not
required for its emergence \citep{https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1365-3113.2009.00504.x}.

Nevertheless, the exact sequence and nature of evolutionary events that led to
the emergence of the gravity-based waggle dance is not known in detail. It is
also not clear how the transition from one version of the code to the other
could happen gradually, while maintaining communicative success at all stages,
and how this process interacted with the change in the orientation of
honeycombs. Because the code is shared, such a transition poses a coordination
problem: a change adopted by senders but not matched by the receivers'
interpretation, or the reverse, disrupts communication, so that intermediate
stages risk a loss of communicative success.

The present study investigates this transition by means of a
computational model of evolving populations of bee-like agents. We
model selection at the level of colonies, and individual behavior at
the level of worker bees as colony members. We address two questions:
under what ecological conditions a direct-pointing referential code
emerges and stabilizes, and what conditions permit a gradual
transition from this code to its gravity-referenced variant. Our aim is
not to reconstruct the precise historical sequence of these changes,
but to delineate the conditions under which such a transition can
proceed without a breakdown of communication.

Our starting point is colonies with horizontal combs, which show some
genomic variation in traits controlling the inclination to direct
signaling and attending to signals. Here we show that communication via
direct pointing evolves readily when the food distribution makes food
moderately hard to find via random search alone.

In the second stage we introduce extrinsic evolutionary pressure for
vertical combs, which degrades the signal of the direct-pointing
code. We show that in this scenario, the following factors correlate with
 the evolutionary transition towards gravity referenced code:
\begin{itemize}
    \item the magnitude of the benefit of vertical combs
    \item the mutation rate
    \item the strength of the coupling between the signaling behavior
      between senders and receivers
\end{itemize}

The pattern of results from these simulations indicates that a basic
referential code based on iconic, direct pointing signal evolves
readily given favorable ecological conditions. On the other hand, the
transition from this basic ancestral system  to the more abstract code
based on gravity referenced pointing only happens under a narrow set
of circumstances involving the confluence of several key favorable
ecological and evolutionary factors.

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Communication in agent societies}
A long line of computational work has studied how communication
systems can emerge and stabilize in populations of interacting agents.
The signaling game introduced by \citet{lewis1969convention} provides
the formal foundation for much of this work, framing a convention of
meaning as a solution to a recurring coordination problem between a
sender and a receiver. \citet{skyrms2010signals} shows how such
signaling conventions can arise without prior agreement through
evolutionary and reinforcement-learning dynamics. Agent-based models
in this tradition have demonstrated that populations playing repeated
language games can self-organize shared, grounded vocabularies and
categories \citep{steels2011modeling}. Laboratory experiments on
iterated learning further show that languages transmitted repeatedly
across learners become progressively more learnable and structured
\citep{kirby2008cumulative}. More recently, deep multi-agent
reinforcement learning has been used to show that discrete
communication protocols can emerge when agents are trained on
cooperative referential tasks \citep{lazaridou2017multi}.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
Our  model is inspired by bee colonies and their waggle dance, but it is
simplified in many important ways, as our goal was not to model bee biology in
detail but rather investigate scenarios for the emergence and evolution of
referential communicative codes, adopting the case of the waggle dance as a way
to ground the modeling in the real world and ensuring that it evolutionarily
plausible.

We model evolution at the level of colonies, and behavior at the level of
individual bee-like agents. There is no sexual reproduction: colonies reproduce
without genetic input from other colonies. Colonies have a number of heritable
traits which are transmitted to offspring colonies subject to mutation, and
individual agents express these traits with some non-heritable individual
variation.

\subsection{Horizontal comb}
The first basic version of the model keeps combs horizontal. We use this version
in the experiment investigating the emergence of the direct pointing code.

\subsection{Evolution of vertical combs}
In the second version of the model we add a selective pressure for vertical
combs, and enable the colony-level trait {\sc comb-tilt}. We use this model to
investigate the transition from the direct pointing code to the
gravity-referenced code.
