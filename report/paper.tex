\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Communication in animal species takes many forms, from the chemical
signals of ants to the symbolic, compositional language of
humans. Each of these communication systems has its own idiosyncratic
properties. What ecological and evolutionary pressures shaped these
systems has been of substantial interest to biologists and cognitive
scientists.  Communication in general assumes some kind of shared code
between senders and receivers. The emergence of such a code
requires some degree of alignment between these two roles.
Likewise, any change or modification to the code needs to be
coordinated between senders and receivers if the breakdown of
communication is to be avoided.

In many cases it is possible to think of a plausible pathway for the
coordinated emergence and development of a shared code, but a verbally
formulated scenario leaves many details underspecified and makes it
hard to be precise about the specific conditions that make code change
trajectories feasible. For this reason computational models of the
evolution of communication systems can provide insights which are hard to
obtain by other means. Such models have been used extensively to study
the emergence of communication and language (\Cref{sec:related}). In
this paper we model a transition of a particularly intriguing
communicative system in invertebrates: the waggle dance of honey bees.

Communication is referential when signals stand for entities or states in the
world rather than merely expressing the signaler's internal state. The
communicative code of Apis species \cite{vonFrisch1967DanceLanguage} is a unique
referential system where food sites discovered by foraging scouts are indicated
to other members of the colony by means of a figure-of-eight dance whose axis
encodes the direction of the food source, and whose duration stands for its
distance. In this study we focus on  whether and how the dances conveys direction.
Different species of Apis vary in the specific dance variant used to
encode the direction of the food patch: in species with exposed horizontal combs
(\emph{Apis florea} and \emph{Apis dorsata}) the bees orient the axis of the
waggle dance directly towards the food patch. In other members of the Apis
genus, combs are vertical and in some species also hidden in cavities. Such
combs make direct pointing impossible, and these species use gravity as a
reference point, and map the azimuth of the food patch relative to the sun onto
the angle of the waggle dance with respect to the vertical. \Cref{fig:waggle}
illustrates these two versions of the waggle dance.\footnote{ This a somewhat
simplified overview: in individual species there may be considerable behavioral
flexibility in how the dance is performed: e.g.\ in Apis mellifera, sometimes
bees dance on a horizontal platform at the entrance to the beehive and this case
they use direct pointing, even though this species generally employs the gravity
code \citep{Esch2012}.} 

The direct-pointing variant is iconic:
the form of the signal, the direction of the waggle axis, mirrors the direction
of the referent. The gravity-referenced variant is more abstract, relying on an
indirect mapping of the sun's azimuth onto an angle measured from the vertical.
The waggle dance thus offers a rare opportunity to study how a referential code
can shift from an iconic to a more abstract form.

The distribution of comb orientation and waggle dance code throughout the Apis
phylogeny (\Cref{fig:phylo}) suggest that the horizontal comb and direct
pointing are the ancestral state (extant in stem Micrapis subgenus). Vertical
combs and the gravity-referenced code are derived traits, present in the other
two Apis subgenerat. Additionally, species within the \emph{Apis} sensu stricto
subgenus are cavity nesters. This pattern indicates that the
gravity code was linked to vertical combs and that cavity nesting is not
required for its emergence \citep{https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1365-3113.2009.00504.x}.

Nevertheless, the exact sequence and nature of evolutionary events that led to
the emergence of the gravity-based waggle dance is not known in detail. It is
also not clear how the transition from one version of the code to the other
could happen gradually, while maintaining communicative success at all stages,
and how this process interacted with the change in the orientation of
honeycombs. Because the code is shared, such a transition poses a coordination
problem: a change adopted by senders but not matched by the receivers'
interpretation, or the reverse, disrupts communication, so that intermediate
stages risk a loss of communicative success.

The present study investigates this transition by means of a
computational model of evolving populations of bee-like agents. We
model selection at the level of colonies, and individual behavior at
the level of worker bees as colony members. We address two questions:
under what ecological conditions a direct-pointing referential code
emerges and stabilizes, and what conditions permit a gradual
transition from this code to its gravity-referenced variant. Our aim is
not to reconstruct the precise historical sequence of these changes,
but to delineate the conditions under which such a transition can
proceed without a breakdown of communication.

Our starting point is colonies with horizontal combs, which show some
genomic variation in traits controlling the inclination to direct
signaling and attending to signals. Here we show that communication via
direct pointing evolves readily when the food distribution makes food
moderately hard to find via random search alone.

In the second stage we introduce extrinsic evolutionary pressure for
vertical combs, which degrades the signal of the direct-pointing
code. We show that in this scenario, the following factors correlate with
 the evolutionary transition towards gravity referenced code:
\begin{itemize}
    \item the magnitude of the benefit of vertical combs
    \item the mutation rate
    \item the strength of the coupling between the signaling behavior
      between senders and receivers
\end{itemize}

The pattern of results from these simulations indicates that a basic
referential code based on iconic, direct pointing signal evolves
readily given favorable ecological conditions. On the other hand, the
transition from this basic ancestral system  to the more abstract code
based on gravity referenced pointing only happens under a narrow set
of circumstances involving the confluence of several key favorable
ecological and evolutionary factors.

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Communication in agent societies}
A long line of computational work has studied how communication
systems can emerge and stabilize in populations of interacting agents.
The signaling game introduced by \citet{lewis1969convention} provides
the formal foundation for much of this work, framing a convention of
meaning as a solution to a recurring coordination problem between a
sender and a receiver. \citet{skyrms2010signals} shows how such
signaling conventions can arise without prior agreement through
evolutionary and reinforcement-learning dynamics. Agent-based models
in this tradition have demonstrated that populations playing repeated
language games can self-organize shared, grounded vocabularies and
categories \citep{steels2011modeling}. Laboratory experiments on
iterated learning further show that languages transmitted repeatedly
across learners become progressively more learnable and structured
\citep{kirby2008cumulative}. More recently, deep multi-agent
reinforcement learning has been used to show that discrete
communication protocols can emerge when agents are trained on
cooperative referential tasks \citep{lazaridou2017multi}.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
Our  model is inspired by bee colonies and their waggle dance, but it is
simplified in many important ways, as our goal was not to model bee biology in
detail but rather investigate scenarios for the emergence and evolution of
referential communicative codes, adopting the case of the waggle dance as a way
to ground the modeling in the real world and ensuring that it evolutionarily
plausible.

We model evolution at the level of colonies, and behavior at the level of
individual bee-like agents. There is no sexual reproduction: colonies reproduce
without genetic input from other colonies. Colonies have a number of heritable
traits which are transmitted to offspring colonies subject to mutation, and
individual agents express these traits with some non-heritable individual
variation. For simplicity and focus, we model the waggle dance as only signaling 
the direction of a food site: our agents do not indicate distance.

\subsection{Colonies, workers, and traits}
\label{sec:traits}
A colony is described by a small set of heritable, real-valued traits. We
introduce here only the traits needed for the basic horizontal-comb model
(\Cref{sec:horizontal}); the additional traits and geometry required for tilted
combs are deferred to \Cref{sec:vertical}.

Three traits govern the behavior of a colony's workers. The \emph{directional
bias} $b$ controls how precisely a dance points; the \emph{receiver attention}
$a$ is the propensity to attend to a dance rather than to search at random; and
the \emph{search limit} $\ell$ is the maximum distance a worker is willing to
travel. The first two are bounded to $[0,1]$ and the search limit to $[0,D]$,
where $D$ is the maximum search distance, a fixed model parameter (set to
$D = 8$ in all our experiments) expressed in arbitrary distance units.

Workers express the colony traits with non-heritable individual variation. A
worker $i$ realizes each trait $x \in \{b, a, \ell\}$ as
\begin{equation}
  x_i = \mathrm{clip}\bigl(x + \varepsilon_i\bigr), \qquad
  \varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \eta^2),
\end{equation}
where $\eta$ is a fixed worker-variation scale and $\mathrm{clip}$ returns the
value to its admissible range. For the search limit, whose range is $[0,D]$
rather than $[0,1]$, the scale is multiplied by $D$, so that $\varepsilon_i \sim
\mathcal{N}(0, (\eta D)^2)$. A colony
thus behaves as a noisy ensemble of workers sharing the same underlying
genotype.

\subsection{Producing and interpreting signals}
\label{sec:signals}
On a horizontal comb the dance points directly at the food: this is the iconic,
direct-pointing code. A worker that has just foraged successfully at azimuth $d$
produces a dance whose intended direction is $d$ itself. The emitted signal is
drawn from a von Mises distribution centered on $d$ with concentration
$\kappa = b_i\,\kappa_{\max}$, plus a small wrapped Gaussian dance noise. The von
Mises distribution is the circular analogue of a Gaussian: a signal $\theta$
centered on $\mu$ with concentration $\kappa$ has density
\begin{equation}
  f(\theta;\mu,\kappa) = \frac{\exp\bigl(\kappa\cos(\theta-\mu)\bigr)}{2\pi I_0(\kappa)},
\end{equation}
where $I_0$ is the modified Bessel function of order zero and $\kappa$ plays the
role of an inverse variance; here $\mu = d$ and $\kappa = b_i\,\kappa_{\max}$.
The directional bias $b$ therefore
controls the sharpness of the dance: a low bias leads to a near-uniform,
uninformative signal, while a high bias gives rise to a tightly concentrated one that
reliably indicates $d$.

A worker that follows a dance reads its direction as a search direction, subject
to a small added interpretation noise. Communication is successful when this
recovered direction is close enough to a food site to lead the follower to it.

\subsection{Foraging environment}
\label{sec:environment}
A colony is evaluated over a fixed number of independent foraging episodes, and
its fitness is averaged over them (\Cref{sec:foraging}). Each episode draws a
fresh environment (\Cref{fig:environment}). A fixed number of food sites are
placed at azimuths drawn
uniformly around the colony and at distances drawn uniformly from
$[d_{\min}, d_{\max}] \subseteq [0, D]$, where $D$ is the maximum search distance
of \Cref{sec:traits}; every site has the same angular width, value $v$, and
finite capacity, the last limiting how many foragers it can supply before being
exhausted. These are held fixed across our experiments, with angular width
$0.2$, value $v = 1$, and capacity $6$.

Each episode also fixes the position of the sun, which serves as the external
reference for the gravity-based code introduced in \Cref{sec:vertical}. The
sun's azimuth is drawn uniformly from an arc of width $\Delta_{\mathrm{sun}}$
centered on a fixed heading $\mu_{\mathrm{sun}}$ (in our experiments
$\mu_{\mathrm{sun}} = \pi/2$ and $\Delta_{\mathrm{sun}} = \pi$), so it is constant
within an episode but varies between episodes. Within an episode the same sun is
shared by every worker, so a dancer and a follower always read the
gravity-referenced code against an identical reference: the sun azimuth enters
encoding and decoding with opposite sign and cancels, and communication is
therefore unaffected by where the sun happens to fall.

\subsection{Foraging and colony payoff}
\label{sec:foraging}
Within each episode (\Cref{sec:environment}) the colony makes a sequence of
foraging attempts. On each attempt a worker is drawn at random; if at least one
dance has already been performed in the current episode,
the worker follows a randomly chosen dance with probability equal to its
attention $a_i$, decoding it into a search direction, and otherwise searches in a
uniformly random direction. The worker locates the nearest food site whose
direction falls within its angular width of the search direction and whose
distance is within the worker's search limit $\ell_i$, provided such a site with
remaining capacity exists.

Payoff accrues per episode. A successful forage yields the food site's value $v$, minus
a travel cost proportional to the site's distance, and prompts the worker to
perform a dance advertising the site to later foragers, at a cost
$c_{\mathrm{base}} + c_{\mathrm{cue}}\,b_i$ that grows with the precision of the
dance. A failed forage costs travel proportional to the full search limit
$\ell_i$, and each act of attending to a dance incurs an attention cost.
Collecting these terms, an episode with successful attempts $S$ (each at distance
$r_k$, performed by a worker with bias $b_k$), failed attempts $F$ (each with
search limit $\ell_j$), and $n_a$ dance-following attempts yields raw payoff
\begin{equation}
  \pi_{\mathrm{raw}} = \sum_{k \in S}\bigl(v - c_{\mathrm{travel}}\,r_k\bigr)
    - \sum_{k \in S}\bigl(c_{\mathrm{base}} + c_{\mathrm{cue}}\,b_k\bigr)
    - \sum_{j \in F} c_{\mathrm{travel}}\,\ell_j
    - c_{\mathrm{att}}\,n_a,
\end{equation}
where the dance-cost sum runs over $S$ because one dance is produced per success.
The scaled episode payoff is $\pi = \bigl(1 + B\,m(\gamma)\bigr)\,\pi_{\mathrm{raw}}$,
with the vertical-comb modifier $m(\gamma) = \gamma$ by default
(\Cref{sec:vertical}). A colony's fitness is its mean episode payoff (floored at a
small positive value).
For diagnostic purposes we also record a \emph{recruitment advantage}: the
difference between the success rate of workers that followed a dance and that of
contemporaneous workers that searched at random while dances were available. A
positive recruitment advantage indicates that the code is functioning, since
following a dance then beats searching blindly.

\subsection{Evolutionary dynamics}
\label{sec:evolution}
Evolution proceeds in non-overlapping generations over a fixed-size population of
colonies; there is no sexual reproduction. In each generation every colony is
evaluated, and the next generation is produced by repeated 
selection of parent colonies with a probability proportional to fitness. 
A selected colony's traits are copied subject to
mutation. Each trait is perturbed by an independent Gaussian step of standard
deviation $\sigma_m$ (scaled by $D$ for the search limit) and then clipped to its
range. The workers of each offspring colony are then regenerated from its traits
as in \Cref{sec:traits}. The mutation rate $\sigma_m$ thus sets the pace of
evolutionary change.

\subsection{Horizontal comb}
\label{sec:horizontal}
The first version of the model is exactly the one described above: the comb is
horizontal and the only code available is direct pointing. We use it to
investigate the conditions under which a referential code emerges and stabilizes,
starting from an initial population with little communicative ability --- low
directional bias and low attention --- so that early foragers signal weakly and
rarely attend to one another.

\subsection{Evolution of vertical combs}
\label{sec:vertical}
The second version of the model adds the machinery needed for tilted combs and
the gravity-referenced code. It introduces two further
colony-level traits describing the nest: the \emph{comb tilt} $\gamma \in [0,1]$,
where $\gamma = 0$ is a horizontal comb and $\gamma = 1$ a vertical one, and the
\emph{comb orientation} $\phi$, the compass heading the comb faces. Like the
behavioral traits these are heritable and mutate by an independent Gaussian step
each generation, but they are
properties of the nest and so are shared by all of a colony's workers rather than
expressed with individual variation. Because the orientation $\phi$ is an angle
defined on a circle of period $P$ (the full circle $\tau$, or $\pi$ when the comb
is treated as axially symmetric), its mutation step has standard deviation
$\sigma_m P$ rather than $\sigma_m$.

\paragraph{Comb geometry.}
The comb is a flat surface on which dances are performed, and its tilt determines
which reference directions a dance can use. We represent the comb by its unit
normal --- the direction perpendicular to its face. When the comb is horizontal
this normal points straight up; as the comb tilts, the normal swings down toward
the horizon in the compass direction $\phi$, until on a vertical comb it lies
flat. Writing the tilt as a physical angle $\theta = \gamma\,\pi/2$ (so
$\theta = 0$ for a horizontal comb and $\theta = \pi/2$ for a vertical one), the
normal has eastward, northward, and upward components
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{n} = \bigl(\,
    \underbrace{\sin\theta\cos\phi}_{\text{east}},\;
    \underbrace{\sin\theta\sin\phi}_{\text{north}},\;
    \underbrace{\cos\theta}_{\text{up}}
  \,\bigr).
\end{equation}
At $\theta = 0$ this reduces to $(0,0,1)$, pointing straight up, and at
$\theta = \pi/2$ it lies in the horizontal plane pointing along $\phi$. A signal
is an angle measured within the comb's surface, and the two codes differ in the
reference direction, lying in that surface, against which the angle is read
(\Cref{fig:comb-codes}).

\paragraph{The two codes.}
In the \emph{direct} (iconic) code the worker takes the world-space direction of
the food as a three-dimensional unit vector,
$\mathbf{f}_d = (\cos d, \sin d, 0)$ for food azimuth $d$ (the vertical component
is zero because food lies in the horizontal plane), and projects
it onto the comb surface, reading off the resulting in-plane angle. How well this
works depends on how much of the food direction survives the projection, which we
measure by the length of the projected vector,
\begin{equation}
  s_{\mathrm{dir}} = \sqrt{1 - \sin^2\theta\,\cos^2(d-\phi)}.
\end{equation}
On a horizontal comb $s_{\mathrm{dir}} = 1$: the food direction lies entirely
within the surface and is represented faithfully. On a tilted comb it shrinks,
and vanishes for food lying in the direction the comb faces, whose horizontal
heading is then lost in the projection. In the \emph{gravity-referenced} code the
worker instead projects the vertical (gravity) direction
$\mathbf{g} = (0,0,1)$, likewise a world-space vector,
onto the comb surface to obtain a reference direction, and encodes the food's
azimuth measured relative to the sun's azimuth as an angle from that reference.
The analogous reliability is
\begin{equation}
  s_{\mathrm{grav}} = \sin\theta = \sin(\gamma\,\pi/2),
\end{equation}
exactly complementary to the direct code's: it is zero on a horizontal comb,
where gravity is perpendicular to the surface and provides no in-plane reference,
and grows to $1$ as the comb becomes vertical. A horizontal comb thus supports
only direct pointing, whereas a vertical comb makes the gravity reference fully
available. When a channel's
strength falls below $1$, the direction it recovers is correspondingly degraded,
modeled as a circular interpolation between the true angle and a uniformly random
one, with weight equal to the strength.

\paragraph{Transposition and blending the codes.}
Which code a worker uses is governed by two further heritable behavioral traits,
the \emph{sender transposition} $t_s$ and \emph{receiver transposition} $t_r$
(both in $[0,1]$ and expressed with individual variation as in
\Cref{sec:traits}). These set how much weight a worker places on the
gravity-referenced code rather than the direct code when, respectively, producing
and interpreting a dance. Let $\delta_{\mathrm{dir}}$ and $\delta_{\mathrm{grav}}$
be the in-plane angles the two codes assign to the food just visited. The
intended dance direction $\delta$ is a weighted circular mean of the two
(\Cref{fig:comb-codes}):
each angle is turned into a unit vector, scaled by its weight, the two vectors
are summed, and $\delta$ is the heading of the resultant,
\begin{equation}
  \delta = \operatorname{atan2}\Bigl(\textstyle\sum_k w_k \sin\delta_k,\;
    \sum_k w_k \cos\delta_k\Bigr), \qquad k \in \{\mathrm{dir}, \mathrm{grav}\}.
\end{equation}
The weights combine the
worker's transposition with each channel's geometric strength,
\begin{equation}
  w_{\mathrm{dir}} = (1 - t_{s,i})\,s_{\mathrm{dir}}, \qquad
  w_{\mathrm{grav}} = t_{s,i}\,s_{\mathrm{grav}}.
\end{equation}
A worker with $t_{s,i} = 0$ therefore signals purely by direct pointing and one
with $t_{s,i} = 1$ purely by the gravity reference, while the strength factors
ensure that a code with no geometric support (such as the gravity code on a
horizontal comb) contributes nothing regardless of $t_s$. The signal is then
emitted from a von Mises distribution about $\delta$ as in \Cref{sec:signals}. A
receiver inverts this process, decoding the observed signal under both codes and
recombining the two recovered world directions by the same kind of weighted
circular mean, now with weights $(1 - t_{r,i})\,s_{\mathrm{dir}}$ and
$t_{r,i}\,s_{\mathrm{grav}}$. Successful communication therefore requires senders
and receivers to use compatible codes: a dance produced under the gravity
reference is understood correctly only by a receiver who also decodes it that
way.

\paragraph{Coupling of sender and receiver.}
Because a working code must change on both sides at once, we allow the mutations
of $t_s$ and $t_r$ to be correlated. Their mutational steps are drawn as a
correlated Gaussian pair with correlation coefficient $\rho$,
\begin{equation}
  \Delta t_s = z_1, \qquad
  \Delta t_r = \rho\,z_1 + \sqrt{1-\rho^2}\;z_2, \qquad
  z_1, z_2 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_m^2),
\end{equation}
which leaves the marginal mutation scale of each trait unchanged while tuning how
tightly the two co-vary. When $\rho$ is high, a mutation that pushes senders
toward the gravity code tends to push receivers by the same amount, so the two
sides of the code can shift in a coordinated fashion rather than drifting apart;
when $\rho = 0$ they evolve independently. The comb tilt $\gamma$, held fixed at
$0$ in the horizontal model, is free to mutate in this version.

\paragraph{Selection for vertical combs.}
Finally, this version adds an extrinsic ecological pressure favoring vertical
combs, unrelated to communication. The raw episode payoff of \Cref{sec:foraging}
is scaled by a factor
\begin{equation}
  1 + B\,\gamma,
\end{equation}
where $B \ge 0$ is the magnitude of the vertical-comb benefit, so that taller
combs earn proportionally more. As $\gamma$ increases under this pressure the
direct channel degrades while the gravity channel becomes available, so
maintaining communicative success requires the population to migrate from the
direct to the gravity-referenced code. Because that shift must be matched on both
the sender and receiver sides, it constitutes the coordination problem of
interest, and we use this version to investigate how the benefit magnitude $B$,
the mutation rate $\sigma_m$, and the sender--receiver coupling $\rho$ govern
whether the transition proceeds without a breakdown of communication.
