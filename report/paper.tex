\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Communication in animal species takes many forms, from the chemical
signals of ants to the symbolic, compositional language of
humans. Each of these communication systems has its own idiosyncratic
properties. What ecological and evolutionary pressures shaped these
systems has been of substantial interest to biologists and cognitive
scientists.  Communication in general assumes some kind of shared code
between senders and receivers. The emergence of such a code this
requires some degree of alignment between the these two roles.
Likewise, any change or modification to the code needs to be
coordinated between senders and receivers if the breakdown of
communication is to be avoided.

In many cases it is possible to think of a plausible pathway for the
coordinated emergence and development of a shared code, but a verbally
formulated scenario leaves many details underspecified and make it
hard to be precise about the specific conditions code change
trajectories feasible. For this reason computational models of the
evolution of communication systems can provide insights which hard to
obtain by other means. In this paper we model a transition of a
particularly intriguing communicative system in invertebrates: the
waggle dance of honey bees.

The communicative code of Apis species \cite{vonFrisch1967DanceLanguage} is
unique system where food sites discovered by foraging scouts are
indicated to other members of the colony by means of a figure-of-eight
dance whose axis encodes the direction of the food source, and whose
duration stands for its distance. Different species of Apis vary in the
specific dance variant used to encode the direction of the food patch: in
open-nesting species (\emph{Apis florea} and \emph{Apis dorsata}) the
bees can perceive the sun and thus can orient the axis of the waggle
dance directly towards the food patch. In cavity nesting species such
as \emph{Apis mellifera}, the worker bees do not see the sun, and their combs
are vertical. They thus use gravity as a reference point, and map the
azimuth of the food patch relative to the sun onto the angle of the waggle
dance with respect to the vertical (see \Cref{fig:waggle}).

The distribution of comb orientation and waggle dance code throughout
the Apis phylogeny (\Cref{fig:phylo}) suggest that the horizontal comb
and direct pointing are the ancestral state (extant in the Micrapis
subgenus). Cavity nesting, vertical combs and the gravity-referenced
code are derived traits (present in the \emph{Apis} sensu stricto
subgenus). The stem bees of the subgenus Mircrapis, on the other hand,
perform the direct pointing dance. 
Nevertheless, the exact sequence of evolutionary events that led
to the emergence of the gravity-based waggle dance is not known. It is
also not clear how the transition from one version of the code to the
other could happen gradually, while maintaining communicative success
at all stages, and how this process interacted with the change in the
orientation of honeycombs.

The present study investigates this transition by means of a
computational model of evolving populations of bee-like agents. We
model selection at the level of colonies, and individual behavior at
the level of worker bees as colony members.

Our starting point are colonies with horizontal combs, which show some
genomic variation in traits controling the inclination to direct
signaling and attending to signals. In this initial stage we
investigate under what circumstances a direct pointing code evolves
and stabilizes. We show that on horizontal combs, communication via
direct pointing evolves readily when food distribution makes it
moderately hard to find it via random search only.

In the second stage we introduce extrinsic evolutionary pressure for
vertical combs, which degrades the signal of the direct-pointing
code. We show that in this scenario, the following factors correlate with
 the evolutionary transition towards gravity referenced code:
\begin{itemize}
    \item the magnitude of the benefit of vertical combs
    \item the mutation rate
    \item the strength of the coupling between the signaling behavior
      between senders and receivers
\end{itemize}

The pattern of results from these simulations indicates that a basic
referential code based on iconic, direct pointing signal evolves
readily given favorable ecological conditions. On the other hand, the
transition from this basic ancestral system  to the more abstract code
based on gravity referenced pointing only happens under a narrow set
of circumstances involving the confluence of several key favorable
ecological and evolutionary factors.
